> [!IMPORTANT]
> Well-documented and well-explained physiological phenomenon. Astronauts in space see bright flashes when high-energy heavy ions (galactic cosmic rays) pass through the visual system, exciting the retina or visual cortex. First systematically reported by Apollo 11 crew, studied through Apollo, Skylab, ISS, and beyond.

## Definition

Brief, bright visual flashes experienced by astronauts in space, especially in dark conditions (eyes closed, sleeping, low ambient light). Caused by direct interaction of heavy charged particles (Z > 6) with neural tissue in the visual pathway.

## Incidents matching

- [[Incident - 1972 Apollo 17 Fragment Field]] (Cernan's "bright spot that flashed right between my eyes like a very bright headlight" descriptions are textbook phosphene reports, not external object observations)

## Significance

Any report from an Apollo or later crewed-spaceflight transcript that describes "flashes between the eyes," "bright spots while trying to sleep," or "flashes lighting up the horizon" during eye-closed periods should be considered phosphene-consistent unless there is independent corroboration of an external source.
