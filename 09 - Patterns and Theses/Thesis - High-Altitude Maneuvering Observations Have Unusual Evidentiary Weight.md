## Hypothesis

Observations of objects ==maneuvering at altitudes substantially above the maximum service ceiling of known manned aircraft== (~85,000 ft for SR-71, ~78,000 ft for U-2) carry unusual evidentiary weight because conventional explanations are sharply constrained at those altitudes. Most candidate platforms (balloons, satellites, debris) do not maneuver in patterns like circles, corkscrews, or 90-degree turns.

## Reasoning

1. Below ~70,000 ft, conventional explanations multiply: commercial and military aircraft, drones, balloons, atmospheric phenomena, sensor artifacts.
2. Above ~80,000 ft, the candidate set shrinks: weather balloons (do not maneuver), satellites (orbital mechanics constrain motion), upper-stage debris (passive ballistic), high-altitude reconnaissance aircraft (limited types, well-cataloged).
3. Maneuvering motion at such altitudes is anomalous against any candidate.

## Supporting incidents

- [[Incident - 1994-01-27 Kazakhstan 747SP]] (contrails estimated at ~100,000 ft showing circles, corkscrews, 90-degree turns)

## Disconfirming evidence

- Altitude estimation by ground or air-based visual observation is notoriously unreliable; the 100,000 ft estimate in the Kazakhstan incident was made by a 747SP crew looking up, which carries large error bars.
- Sensor artifacts can mimic maneuvering behavior even when the underlying object is stationary or following simpler paths.

## Confidence

confidence:: moderate
basis:: Logic chain is sound; practical application limited by altitude estimation uncertainty in most non-instrumented observations.

## Implication

When evaluating future UAP observations, ==altitude is a powerful filter==. An observation at FL250 has many candidate explanations; an observation credibly at FL900-FL1000 has few. Prioritize observations that include radar altimeter, triangulation, or other instrumented altitude measurements.
